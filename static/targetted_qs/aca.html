Government/direct regulation 4. No regulation. If no explicit comment on this issue, return an empty object. To help you understand the meanings of the different forms of regulation for this task, here is an overview of each:

Self-regulation

Self-regulation is generally characterised by industry-formulated rules and codes of conduct, with industry solely responsible for enforcement. For example, the content of advertising is subject to a self-regulatory system created by the Australian Association of National Advertisers (AANA) in 1998. The AANA established a Code of Ethics. The related organisation Ad Standards operates a community panel that hears complaints regarding advertising content. Similarly, the Australian Press Council (APC) administers a self-regulatory scheme for print and online news. APC formulates principles and standards and takes complaints from the public, which are heard by adjudication panels. The 'classification' of audio material is also self-regulated under the Recorded Music Labelling Code of Practice. There is no legislation, and individual record companies are responsible for labelling recordings under a code that outlines labelling provisions and establishes a complaints handling mechanism.

Co-regulation

Co-regulation typically refers to situations where industry develops and administers its own arrangements, but government provides legislative backing to enable the arrangements to be enforced. Regulation of radio and television content is co-regulatory. Various industry groups have developed codes under Part 9 of the Broadcasting Services Act 1992 (Cth). Most aspects of program content are governed by these codes, which include the Commercial Television Industry Code of Practice and the Commercial Radio Australia Code of Practice and Guidelines. Once implemented, the ACMA monitors these codes and deals with unresolved complaints made under them. A similar scheme applies to telecommunications codes developed under Part 6 of the Telecommunications Act 1997 (Cth), such as the Telecommunications Consumer Protections Code.

Direct/government regulation

Direct government regulation comprises primary and subordinate legislation as well as rules developed by a government regulator. It is the most commonly used form of regulation. Examples of direct government regulation are: industry standards developed under Part 6 of the Telecommunications Act 1997 (Cth); programs standards developed under Part 9 of the Broadcasting Services Act 1992 (Cth); the Broadcasting Services (Events) Notice 2023; the Online Safety (Basic Online Safety Expectations) Determination 2022; Guidelines for the Classification of Films 2012.

No regulation

No regulation is where there is no form of self-regulation, co-regulation or direct/government regulation. It includes situations where a company develops terms of use, community guidelines or some other form of rule that applies only to itself. We refer to this last type of arrangement as 'governance', but for this exercise, it can be classified as 'no regulation'.
